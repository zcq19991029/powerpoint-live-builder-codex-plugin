# General lesson-preparation workflow

Use this workflow for any subject. Adapt it to the learner level and lesson duration instead of forcing every section into every deck.

## Required discovery

- Subject and exact lesson topic
- Learner level and prerequisite knowledge
- Classroom, trial lesson, review lesson, training, or self-study context
- Available teaching time
- Learning objectives and assessment expectations
- Key points and difficult/misconception-prone points
- Required source material, curriculum standard, school identity, and visual style
- Desired page count and interaction/animation behavior

Ask only for missing choices that materially change the lesson. Extract answers from supplied lesson plans, textbooks, HTML, Word files, or existing slides before asking.

## Lesson architecture

Choose a suitable subset and order:

1. Title and lesson promise
2. Situation, question, demonstration, or prior-knowledge activation
3. Learning objectives stated in learner-observable terms
4. Concept construction in small steps
5. Diagram, experiment, example, evidence, or worked model
6. Key rule, formula, process, or language pattern
7. Guided practice with reasoning exposed
8. Common error or misconception correction
9. Independent check, discussion, or application
10. Summary, exit question, and optional assignment

## Slide-level teaching rule

Each slide should have one teaching job. The title should express that job, not merely name a topic. Reveal information in the order a teacher would explain it; do not show the final answer before the reasoning unless the lesson design calls for comparison or prediction.

## Beginner lessons

- Define new symbols and vocabulary before using them.
- Move from concrete/visual to abstract/symbolic.
- Keep steps explicit and avoid unexplained jumps.
- Include at least one worked example and one learner check.

## Visual and animation pedagogy

- Use diagrams to clarify relationships, processes, spatial structures, or changes over time.
- Keep labels editable when possible.
- Use click reveals to control attention and support questioning.
- Group elements that form one semantic unit; do not animate decoration merely to create motion.
- Keep essential information visible long enough for note-taking.

## Final teaching QA

- Objectives, explanation, practice, and exit check align.
- Terminology and notation remain consistent.
- Every example is correct and appropriate for the learner level.
- Common mistakes are explicitly addressed when relevant.
- Font size and contrast are suitable for classroom projection.
- No text covers instructional illustrations.
- Animation order matches the spoken teaching sequence.
